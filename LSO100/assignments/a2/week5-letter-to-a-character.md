**Purpose of the Assignment**

Literature invites readers into the lives, struggles, and experiences of
fictional characters. As we read, we often form opinions about the
choices characters make, sympathize with their situations, question
their decisions, or reflect on how their experiences connect to our own
lives.

This assignment asks you to engage directly with a character from one of
the short stories we have studied so far in the course.

By writing a letter, you will demonstrate your understanding of the
story, its themes, and the character's experiences while developing your
ability to analyze literature through personal and critical reflection.

**Assignment Instructions**

Choose **one character** from any of the stories studied in Weeks 1–5.

You will write a letter addressed to that character.

Your letter should demonstrate that you understand:

- The events of the story

- The character's experiences

- The character's motivations and choices

- The larger themes explored in the text

You may choose to:

- Offer advice

- Ask questions

- Express sympathy or disagreement

- Reflect on the character's decisions

- Discuss how the character's experiences affected you as a reader

- Explain what you think the character should have done differently

- Comment on the social, cultural, or personal issues presented in the
  story

The letter should be thoughtful, respectful, and grounded in the details
of the text.

**Length**

**500–700 words**

**Requirements**

Your letter must:

**1. Address a Specific Character**

Clearly identify the character you are writing to and the story from
which the character comes.

Examples include:

- Piquette from *The Loons*

- Ann from *The Painted Door*

- Vanessa from *To Set Our House in Order*

- The narrator from *Boys and Girls*

- The father or narrator from *The Boat*

- Ellen from *The Lamp at Noon*

- Spit Delaney from *Spit Delaney's Island*

**2. Demonstrate Understanding of the Story**

Show that you understand the events, conflicts, and circumstances that
shape the character's experiences.

Avoid writing a generic letter that could apply to any character.

**3. Include Specific References to the Text**

Support your ideas by referring to specific events, situations, actions,
or moments from the story.

For example, instead of writing:

"You made some bad decisions."

You might write:

"When you chose to leave the farm during the storm despite Paul's
concerns, I understood your frustration, but I also wondered whether the
isolation and harsh conditions had already pushed both of you beyond
your limits."

Specific references demonstrate close reading and engagement with the
text.

**4. Engage Critically with the Character**

Move beyond summary.

Consider questions such as:

- Why did the character behave this way?

- What pressures influenced their decisions?

- What alternatives were available?

- What does the character reveal about the story's themes?

- What might the character learn from their experiences?

**5. Use Letter Format**

Include:

- A greeting

- Body paragraphs

- A closing

Example:

Dear Ann,

...

Sincerely,

\[Your Name\]

**Textual Evidence**

Although this assignment is written as a letter, it should still
demonstrate close engagement with the text.

You are encouraged to refer to important events, conflicts, and
character actions from the story.

Direct quotations are optional but may be used when appropriate.

**Tips for Success**

- Choose a character you find interesting or challenging.

- Revisit important moments in the story before writing.

- Focus on analysis and reflection rather than summary.

- Support your ideas with specific references to the text.

- Consider how the character connects to larger themes discussed in
  class, such as survival, identity, family, isolation, belonging,
  community, or personal responsibility.

The strongest letters will demonstrate both a thoughtful understanding
of the character and a meaningful engagement with the themes of the
story.
